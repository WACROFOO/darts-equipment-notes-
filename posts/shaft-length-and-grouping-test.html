Shaft Length and Grouping: 30 Throws per Length

By TungstenTalk | March 2026

Everyone has an opinion on shaft length. Short shafts for tight grouping. Long shafts for stability. Medium if you cannot decide. I wanted data instead of opinions, so I ran a controlled test.

Same barrel. Same flights. Same session. Thirty rounds of three darts at treble 20 with each shaft length. Measured the spread of every group with calipers on the board.

Test setup

Barrel: Target Bolide 11 (23g, straight, 90% tungsten). I used this barrel because it accepts all three shaft lengths without balance problems - the weight distribution is neutral enough that shaft length becomes the main variable.

Flights: Standard-shaped Condor Axe, 27.5mm. Same flight on all three shafts to eliminate that variable.

Shafts tested:

  • Short: 35mm nylon (Harrows Clic)
  • Medium: 48mm nylon (Harrows Clic)
  • Long: 54mm nylon (Harrows Clic)

All nylon. All same brand. All same locking system. The only variable is the 19mm difference between shortest and longest.

Measurement method: after each three-dart throw at treble 20, I measured the spread from the centre of the leftmost dart to the centre of the rightmost dart, in millimetres. Thirty rounds per shaft. Ninety measurements total.

What the numbers say

Short shafts produced the tightest average grouping by 3.5mm over medium and 6.1mm over long. That is real. But the spread within each shaft length is enormous. My best group with longs (14mm) was tighter than my worst group with shorts (91mm). Session form, fatigue, and focus dominate shaft length as a variable.

The interesting column is standard deviation. Longs had the lowest spread variance at 17.1mm. Shorts had the highest at 22.4mm. What this means: long shafts are more consistent but with a wider average group. Short shafts are less consistent but when they group, they group tight.

Think of it as a trade-off between ceiling and floor. Short shafts raise your ceiling and lower your floor. Long shafts narrow the gap between your best and worst.

Why this happens

Longer shafts move the centre of pressure further behind the centre of mass. That increases the restoring moment - the aerodynamic force that straightens the dart mid-flight. A dart with a longer shaft is harder to destabilise but also harder to correct if your release is off-axis.

Short shafts reduce that restoring moment. The dart is more responsive to your throw. Good release, tight group. Bad release, wide group. There is less aerodynamic forgiveness in the system.

This matches what I see in PDC setups. Players with textbook releases - Luke Humphries, for instance - use short shafts because they do not need forgiveness. Players with more variable actions tend toward medium.

The weight factor

There is a secondary effect I did not expect. The long shaft setup weighed 0.8g more at the rear than the short setup. That shifted the balance point forward by about 2mm. On a 23g dart, a 2mm shift is noticeable - it changes where the dart wants to arc.

If I were being rigorous, I would rerun this test with weighted short shafts to match the total mass. But for a practical test, this is how players actually experience the change. You swap shaft length and you also swap rear-end weight. They are coupled.

My conclusion

Shaft length matters less than shaft length discussions suggest. The difference between my best and worst throws in any single shaft configuration was larger than the difference between shaft configurations. If your grouping is inconsistent, the shaft is not the first thing to change.

That said, if your throw is already repeatable and you want to optimise: short shafts will tighten your average group at the cost of consistency. Long shafts will stabilise your worst throws at the cost of your best. Medium sits in between, which is why most manufacturers ship darts with medium shafts as the default.

The data supports what boring advice always says: try all three lengths for a week each and keep whichever one produces the most consistent scoring over 20+ sessions. Not 3 throws. Not 10. Twenty sessions minimum before you have a sample worth trusting.

I am keeping the medium shafts. My release is not clean enough for shorts to pay off, and the 3.5mm average improvement does not justify the 22.4mm standard deviation. Your mileage may vary. Literally.
